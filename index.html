the parallel record

What the state knew and produced — set alongside what the women experienced and knew. Legislative text and demographic record on the left. First-person testimony on the right. Two kinds of knowledge. One event.

state record — legislative, demographic, juridical
women's testimony — first-person, oral, literary
1857
colonial precondition Census Act, 1881 (precursor logic)

The British colonial census systematically enumerated the Indian population by religion for the first time, producing Hindu, Muslim, and Sikh as administratively discrete categories. These enumerated identities would become the basis for territorial allocation in 1947. The state created the categories through which it would later divide the bodies.

the mark that preceded the wound
"Before the 'body' there is the 'flesh,' that zero degree of social conceptualization that does not escape concealment under the brush of discourse."
— Hortense J. Spillers, "Mama's Baby, Papa's Maybe" (1987), Diacritics 17.2, p. 67
June 1947
colonial legislation Indian Independence Act, 1947 (UK Parliament, 18 July 1947)

The British Parliament legislates the end of the British Raj and the creation of two independent dominions — the Union of India and the Dominion of Pakistan — to take effect on 15 August 1947. Lord Mountbatten accelerates the timeline from 1948 to 1947, compressing over a century of colonial administrative structure into weeks. The Act entrusts the territorial demarcation to the Boundary Commission.

"His Majesty's Government... do hereby declare that as from the fifteenth day of August, nineteen hundred and forty-seven, two independent Dominions shall be set up in India, to be known respectively as India and Pakistan."

Cyril Radcliffe — a British lawyer who had never been to India, who barely knew the geography of Bengal and Punjab — is appointed chairman of both boundary commissions. He is given five weeks. He leaves India before the boundary is published. He never speaks of it again. He does not accept his fee of Rs. 40,000.

women's testimony — on the speed
"They drew a line. They had five weeks to draw a line that would determine the fate of 88 million people. He had never been to India before. He left immediately after."
— Viceroy's House (dir. Gurinder Chadha, 2017) / historical record
Aug 17, 1947
the line published Radcliffe Line — published 17 August 1947, two days after independence

The Radcliffe Line divides British India into India, West Pakistan, and East Pakistan (present-day Bangladesh). It splits Punjab and Bengal — culturally, linguistically, and agriculturally integrated regions — along religious demographic lines drawn from outdated census data. The boundary is published two days after the countries have already declared independence. Millions have already begun moving. The violence has already begun.

14 million people are displaced in the weeks surrounding the announcement — the largest forced migration in recorded history. Estimates of deaths range from 200,000 to 2 million. Between 75,000 and 100,000 women are abducted, raped, forcibly converted, or killed — many by members of their own families, in acts framed as protecting communal honor.

Nov 1948
state legislation — India Inter-Dominion Agreement, 11 November 1948

India and Pakistan sign an Inter-Dominion Agreement for the mutual "recovery and restoration" of abducted persons. The agreement classifies abducted women as recoverable communal property. It defines an "abducted person" as any Muslim woman left in India, or any Hindu or Sikh woman left in Pakistan, after 1 March 1947.

Critically, the agreement pre-classifies all mixed Hindu-Muslim marriages formed after Partition as forced — regardless of the women's own testimony about their marriages, their children, their choices. The women's consent is not a legal variable.

"Abducted person means a male child under the age of sixteen years or a female of any age who is a non-Muslim... and who on or after the 1st day of March, 1947, has been separated from his or her family and is found to be living with or under the control of any other individual or family." — Act No. 65 of 1949, Section 2(a)
testimony — on the recovery operation
"Abducted women were recovered — but recovered for whom? Returned to the family, the community, the nation. Rarely to themselves."
— Ritu Menon & Kamla Bhasin, Borders and Boundaries (Rutgers University Press, 1998), Ch. 2
1949
state legislation — India Abducted Persons (Recovery and Restoration) Act, No. 65 of 1949 (India)

Enacted 28 December 1949. The Act empowers police officers to enter any premises, arrest any woman they "believe or have reason to suspect" is an abducted person, and confine her in a government camp pending "restoration to her relatives." The Act explicitly removes the requirement for a warrant.

Women who had formed new families — including women who had married, borne children, converted by choice — are forcibly removed from their current households. Children born of these unions are left behind when Muslim women are returned to Pakistan. Between January 1954 and September 1957, at least 860 such children are recorded as separated from their mothers in this way.

"A police officer... may take charge of any person who he has reason to believe is an abducted person and deliver that person into the custody of the Camp Officer..." — Section 4, Act No. 65 of 1949

The Act remains in force until 1960. Approximately 30,000 women are "recovered" on the Indian side; Pakistan's numbers are lower and contested.

testimony — on recovery
"She had been 'recovered' three times — once by Pakistani soldiers, once by Indian soldiers, once by her own family. Each time, they told her she was being saved."
— Collected by Urvashi Butalia, The Other Side of Silence (Duke University Press, 2000)
1950
diplomatic legislation Nehru–Liaquat Pact (Delhi Pact), 8 April 1950

Signed between Prime Minister Nehru of India and Prime Minister Liaquat Ali Khan of Pakistan following communal violence in Bengal. The Pact guarantees the rights of minorities in both countries and establishes bilateral machinery for the protection of citizens. Women and their property are treated as part of the demographic management problem the Pact addresses — their rights subordinated to the nation-state's interest in population stabilization.

on what the state calls protection
"The nation, in its very founding, required the sacrifice of women's bodies. This sacrifice was then rendered invisible."
— Urvashi Butalia, "Community, State and Gender," Economic and Political Weekly (jstor.org/stable/4399641)
1951
state legislation — Pakistan Pakistan Citizenship Act, No. II of 1951 (enacted 13 April 1951)

Pakistan's first citizenship law codifies nationality primarily through patrilineal descent. Women cannot at the time of independence transmit their citizenship to their husbands or children in the same way as men. The Act contains a separate section on married women, reflecting the family as the site of national reproduction — where the woman's body is the instrument through which the nation replenishes itself. The kinship system becomes the mechanism of national transmission. The Act is shaped by the problem of Muslim refugees who had migrated from India — many of them women — and whose status needed to be defined as citizens of a nation some had never inhabited.

on women and national transmission
"The family is the site of reproduction of the nation and stands in a metonymic relationship to it. Both countries gave precedence to patrilineal descent and women could not at the time of Independence pass on their citizenship to their husbands or children in the same way as a man."
— Cabeiri Robinson, "Pakistan's Daughter-in-Law," Cairn.info (2014)
1955
state legislation — India Citizenship Act, 1955 (India) — also: Abducted Persons Act expires

India's comprehensive Citizenship Act is passed, codifying jus soli citizenship (by birth). The Abducted Persons (Recovery and Restoration) Act's initial period expires in May 1955, then is extended until 1960 under the Continuance Bill of 1955. At its close, tens of thousands of women's trajectories remain unaccounted for in any official record. The state moves on. The women do not.

1979
state legislation — Pakistan Offence of Zina (Enforcement of Hudood) Ordinance, No. VII of 1979 (Pakistan)

Promulgated under General Zia ul-Haq's Islamization program. The Ordinance criminalizes zina (extramarital sex) and — crucially — classifies rape ("zina-bil-jabr," zina by force) as a subset of zina rather than a distinct crime. Prosecution of rape under hadd (Quranic punishment) requires four adult Muslim male eyewitnesses. The result: women who report rape become liable to prosecution for zina if they cannot produce four witnesses to the non-consensual nature of the intercourse. Thousands of women are imprisoned under the law for "honor" crimes. The Ordinance remains in force until partial reform under the Protection of Women Act, 2006.

"A person is said to commit zina-bil-jabr if he or she has sexual intercourse with a woman or man... to whom he or she is not validly married, in circumstances which constitute rape." — Zina Ordinance, Section 6
on the law that names violation as consent
"The laws rendered most sexual assault victims unable to seek redress through the criminal justice system, deeming them guilty of illegal sex rather than victims of unlawful violence."
— Human Rights Watch, "Pakistan: Reform Hudood Laws Now" (2006)
2019
state legislation — India Citizenship (Amendment) Act, 2019 (India) — implemented March 2024

For the first time in independent India's history, a religious criterion is introduced into the naturalization process. The CAA fast-tracks citizenship for Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, and Christians who fled Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Afghanistan before 31 December 2014 — while explicitly excluding Muslims. The Indian Home Ministry describes it as "compassionate and ameliorative legislation." Critics argue it institutionalizes the same communal grammar the Partition imposed: the Muslim body as always already belonging elsewhere.

In conjunction with the proposed National Register of Citizens (NRC), the CAA risks rendering India's 200 million Muslims stateless if unable to document citizenship. In Assam, a citizenship screening process had already rendered over one million people stateless. On 15 December 2019, hundreds of Muslim women began a sit-in at Shaheen Bagh, Delhi, blocking a highway in protest. They remain there for 101 days. The government describes the protest as a security threat.

"The Citizenship Amendment Act grants Indian nationality to Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Parsis, and Christians who fled to Hindu-majority India due to religious persecution from Muslim-majority Afghanistan, Bangladesh and Pakistan before Dec. 31, 2014." — Reuters, March 2024
the grammar that did not end
"Muslims, whose 'natural home,' they asserted, was now in Pakistan — as opposed to Hindus and Sikhs, who were 'Indian,' even though separated from the mainland..."
— Statelessness and Citizenship Review, "India's Citizenship Laws: A Longue Durée" (2022)
1947–
now
ongoing

The colonial production of knowledge that marked certain bodies as always already suspect continues: in discriminatory citizenship legislation, in the racialization of South Asian Muslim bodies in diaspora, in policing of marriage and reproductive labor to maintain caste and communal purity, in the reproductive coercion placed on women to maintain community boundaries. The categories the Partition institutionalized are not historical residue. They are live structures. The body is still the border.

"The nation, in its very founding, required the sacrifice of women's bodies. This sacrifice was then rendered invisible."

This project takes the 1947 Partition of British India — the administrative division of a subcontinent into India and Pakistan along hastily drawn religious lines — as an onto-epistemological problem: who gets to produce knowledge about what happened, in whose name, and at whose expense?

The archive is structured so that communal identity — whether a voice is Hindu or Muslim, Indian or Pakistani — is disclosed last. You encounter the person before the category. This is not a trick. It is a theoretical position: the refusal of the grammar through which the state sorted bodies into parts.

Existing archival work — Urvashi Butalia's oral histories, Ritu Menon and Kamla Bhasin's testimonial collections — has been essential in recovering women's voices from the margins of nationalist historiography. But these projects largely present women already sorted: named, attributed, positioned within the communal categories the Partition itself produced. What this project refuses is that sorting — asking what becomes legible about shared experience when the state's grammar of differentiation is, even briefly, withheld.

— Sahithi Medikondla. Sex, Gender, and Society. Dartmouth College, 2026.
